Help, my watch has stopped!

If I told you that Bob’s cellphone weighs the same as mine, and you’re skeptical, you can always weigh the two devices and confirm it for yourself. If I told you that Bob’s phone has the same dimensions as mine, once again you don’t have to take my word for it. You can use a ruler and verify. But if I told you that Thais’s icebreaker speech which we just heard lasted exactly as long as my own icebreaker from many years ago, you would have no way of verifying this after the fact. Both events are now history, and it is impossible to time them again.

But it’s even more dire than that. Let alone comparing events that happened years apart, we have no guarantee that the time interval measured by a one-minute jump in my watch is equal to that of the next minute. My watch could be faulty. I could check it against another watch, but that could be faulty too. Is there another way to verify what is being measured by a watch or a clock?

All clocks rely on some repeatable event, and trust that that event always takes the same amount of time every time it happens. But this trust can be misplaced. The first and still most important clock for humans is the earth-sun system. Every apparent circuit of the sun around the earth is defined to be one day, subdivided into 24 hours. But we now know that the earth is slowing down, when compared to other, presumably more reliable clocks. Once an earth day lasted as little as 6 hours! It will eventually be 25, 26 hours and more! Even in the short term, a day’s length varies throughout the year, and a sundial clock tracking the sun doesn’t always get to see the sun.

Mechanical clocks, invented around the year 1270, were more reliable, but they too can drift due to friction, gravity, temperature, corrosion. The oscillations of quartz crystals allows for more accurate timepieces, but they too have to be corrected for temperature. The best clocks right now are atomic clocks, where the repeatable event is the oscillation of the outer electron in a cesium atom.

But what is it that’s happening during or between these oscillations? Sir Isaac Newton confidently bluffed that "absolute, true, and mathematical time flows equably of its own nature, without regard to anything external". It was a very useful bluff though, and we modern humans have internalized it. Any number of pop songs talk mournfully of time being a river flowing into nowhere, at a constant speed of 60 minutes an hour. But this is just a spatial metaphor. Leaving aside the circular definition, what is flowing and where? We may be better off saying time is simply whatever a clock measures, provided it is a good-quality clock. This turns out to be good enough for routine science and technology, though perhaps does not please our soul. For this, let’s see what actually impelled humans to look for accurate clocks in the first place.

The first mechanical clocks had, perhaps strangely, a religious purpose rather than a commercial or even a scientific one. Monks in monasteries developed them to help schedule their prayers so that they could prepare for deliverance. Even in secular settings pocket watches weren’t so much toys or jewelry but memento mori, or reminders of death. A clock didn’t just log the passing of time but was a constant reminder of the winding down of life.

The most awesomely accurate clocks in nature, which existed long before us and will handily outlast us, do the same thing. They mark disintegration and decay. Consider the radioactive decay of carbon 14 atoms. Half of them decay in 5730 years. Half of the remaining half decays in another 5730 years. And so on. How do the carbon 14 atoms know that half of them should say goodbye in 5730 years, and how do they know which half of them should step up to the chopping block? The magic is lessened if, instead of thinking of the decay as happening in time, we equate time to the amount of decay. We say that 5730 years have elapsed when half of the carbon 14 atoms have decayed, just like we said 24 hours have elapsed when the sun made a full apparent circuit of the earth.

Clocks and watches can now be seen as something rather more ominous or tragic, depending on your point of view: They are accurate recorders of the inevitable decay of our part of the universe. A hill collapses on Pluto; a glacier disintegrates on a satellite of Saturn; an asteroid crashes into Mars; there’s a flare on the Sun; there’s one more act of terrorism on Earth; my pencil lead breaks…​ and the sum of all such disintegration is measured by this watch. Every minute corresponds to an equivalent amount of total decay, and this is what is accurately reported by this watch, this tiny little machine that you can buy for forty bucks or less!

Is this a more magical explanation? Perhaps. But then again, perhaps not. Watches, quartz crystals, cesium atoms, our consciousness, are all part of the universe, and it is natural that they take part in the common decay and winding down of the universe, and beat to this collective pulse called time.

And when everything has decayed completely, then what? There is no longer any movement, any change. Everything stops, including the clocks, not just the clinkers but the most accurate ones too, for there is no longer any decay to record. Time itself will then have come to an end.
