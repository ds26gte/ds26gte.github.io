The Bird of Paradise

[I have told you before of the 16th-century palm-leaf manuscripts that I found in an old abandoned building while adventuring about in my hometown Vizag as a young boy. Here is another manuscript from that stash. I have not added or subtracted a single word.]

~

My name is Nina.

I belong to an impecunious but respectable family on the west coast of India. In my 16th year I visited my uncle’s house on Tigerhead island in the Arabian Sea. Tigerhead island is a beautiful green-brown leaf floating on emerald waters, or so it seemed. The only hitch was that the Portuguese, who had recently found a sea route to India from Europe, had established a cantonment on the western edge of the island. Our king had not been able to prevent this, but at least now there was an uneasy peace after some initial hostilities, and each side mostly minded its own business.

One day I went walking in the woods in the interior of Tigerhead when I froze. In the distance I spotted a Portuguese man walking with a notebook and a flagon of water in his hand. He seemed ordinary enough but I was taking no chances. My family had often told me about the wild and disrespectful ways the Portuguese had with women, so I hid by the bole of a tree.

"What are you hiding from, miss? No dangerous animals, I hope?" said a gentle voice in perfect Malayalam. I figured it was one of our neighbors out gathering firewood, but kept my head down.

"No. I’m just careful," I replied, shushing the speaker. "There is a wild Portuguese man at one o’clock."

"Oh?" the voice said, with some amusement.

I looked up, and it was the "wild" Portuguese man. Well, a boy really. I opened my mouth to speak, but no words came.

"How do you speak our language?" I asked finally.

"Oh, that," he waved his hand dismissively. "I am the linguist apprentice on the cantonment, and have been assigned to learn the languages along this coastline."

"You speak it very well," I said, despite my wariness.

"I wish," he replied modestly, "but I’m glad to meet a native speaker. You can help me improve better than any number of books."

He did have a slight accent, and some of his phrases were a little odd. Charming but odd. I knew some of the Portuguese creole, so we were able to converse quite well.

I learned that his name was Guilherme, and that he had run away to sea as a young boy, seeking adventure I suppose. "I want to see the world and learn about life," he said pompously, "so I can write stories that will last forever …​"

He went on and on, and I found it hard to remain afraid of him. He seemed like a harmless idiot. He wasn’t handsome or anything, but there was a geeky curiosity and energy about him that I found amusing.

We would often meet in the woods. I would name for him the various flora and fauna we came across, and the peculiar rock and sea formations that were so new to him. Who knew, maybe he would include them in his stories. Maybe he would include me …​

One day we saw a bird of paradise floating in the southern sky. "What is that?" he asked, mesmerized.

"That’s the male bird of paradise," I said. "They are very rare."

Indeed, there is reputedly only one of them sighted on Tigerhead. Birds of paradise are large birds native to the Indonesian archipelago, which is to the south-east of us. The males are splendidly multicolored: purple, blue, green, yellow, orange, red. We looked at it with the brass magnifying tube that I always had with me. I remembered with some sadness and resentment that it was Europeans who had hunted these trusting birds to the brink of extinction. Europeans like Guilherme. So I did not mention it to him.

As the day approached for me to return to the mainland, Guilherme said he wanted to cook me a goodbye meal in thanks for the help that I’d given him in learning Malayalam. I laughed and said it wasn’t necessary, but he insisted, and I found it flattering and agreed.

When I saw him that day at the makeshift table on the forest floor with the food arrayed on it on plantain leaves, I almost fainted. Despair and anger seized me. Even from a distance, I could make out the colors of the dead bird of paradise: purple, green, orange. Guilherme, stupid cruel Guilherme, had killed the last remaining bird of paradise on Tigerhead for a farewell meal. There he was smiling in innocence, not knowing the extent of the evil that he had done.

I turned and ran.

"Wait, wait," he shouted. "What’s wrong?"

"How could you? I thought you had some heart. But I guess you’re just like all the other plunderers and invaders."

He looked puzzled and hurt. Who cares? That is not my problem. I didn’t want to spend one more second with someone who could kill such a rare and beautiful creature.

I did not go into the woods on the subsequent days. On the day before I was to leave the island, as I was moping in my aunt’s backyard, I saw a bird of paradise on the southern sky. Another one? So soon? I looked closer with my magnifying tube. Unless I was severely mistaken, it was the same one I’d seen with Guilherme.

I quickly ran to the forest, to the place of the table. The food had dispersed by now of course, scavenged or swallowed up by the weather, but there was still some residue. I looked for bird bones and feathers. There were none. Instead I saw fruit pits and husks of grains, and the skins of vegetables both Indian and exotic. I could see the faded colors: purple, green, orange: colors of plants and spices and condiments. Poor Guilherme had not killed a bird of paradise at all.

I worked up my courage and went to the cantonment. I asked for the priest. A padre named Father Varkey, wearing ridiculous yellow tights under his cassock, approached me.

"What is it, my child?" he asked.

"Guilherme," I said. "You have a linguist named Guilherme. My family in the mainland has books for him that he could study. Could I see him?"

I was lying through my teeth of course, but anything, anything to see Guilherme and tell him I was sorry.

"Ah, my child," said Father Varkey, "Guilherme has already taken a ship to East Africa."

"When will he be back?" I asked desperately.

"He won’t be back, child. He is going back to Inglaterra."

I didn’t know what to do. Hoping against hope, I asked, "Do you have his address? My uncle can probably contact him to let him know."

"Certainly," said the padre. He rummaged in his cabin and came back with a piece of paper.

I thanked him and left the compound. As I glanced at the paper, I stopped. This was a mistake. Instead of Guilherme, it had a different name: William something.

"Father," I hoarsely called back. "This isn’t Guilherme’s address. It has some other name on it."

"No, that is the address he gave us. Let me see."

After looking at it for a few moments, he laughed. "Yes, this is indeed Guilherme’s address. Guilherme is an English boy. That is his name in English. They call him William there. It is the same name."

"Oh," I said, surprised but relieved.

I looked again at the precious piece of paper.

William, it said. William Shakespeare.
