Arjun couldn’t believe their fingers had touched, and that Zehra wasn’t pulling hers back.

"Oh, um," he said, as articulation failed him.

"You ok?" asked Zehra, smiling.

"Yes, yes," said Arjun. "I’m just happy, so happy, that we could…​"

"We could what?" asked Zehra, arching an adorable eyebrow.

"Well, this! You don’t know how long I’ve been dreamed of this moment. I never thought it could ever happen. Am I still dreaming?"

"Ah — no. I could pinch you, if you want," and Zehra proceeded to do just that, but gently. They were both very dignified, even though their subtle love for each other would have been obvious to a discerning viewer, had there been any.

"Ouch!" said Arjun, in mock pain. He laughed. Zehra laughed. The trees in the boulevard swayed in the summer breeze. They seemed to laugh too.

"So what do we do now?" asked Zehra.

Arjun was clearly stumped for a reply, so she leaned in and kissed him.

After a bit of this, Arjun said, "Oh! I should get back to the police station, and let them know they can reinstate me, after taking a statement from you that you’ve withdrawn charges. If that’s OK with you."

"Oh, of course, we need you to be gainfully employed! And, after that, you can come by to meet my imam."

"Your imam?"

"Of course, silly. You’re obviously converting, if we’re to get married."

"Married? Are you sure?"

"Why? Are you having second thoughts?"

"No, no. This all seems a bit sudden. For you, I mean, darling. Only yesterday you hated my guts. I want to be sure you’ll be happy."

"I’m sure. You are all green flags to me, Arjun. You cleaned out your retirement to fund this school, got me a job, and helped me out in so many ways. What woman wouldn’t want a man like you?"

Arjun grinned shyly. "I do love you, Zehra. Thank you for accepting me, and letting me finally reduce the burden of guilt, even if its shadow will follow me for the rest of my days."

"Guilt? Oh you mean Kabir. Don’t worry about that. That’s all in the past now. Kabir was a good man, but we need to let him rest in peace."

Arjun was a little nonplussed at her pragmatism, but then figured she was being incredibly brave about the tragedy, a tragedy that he had visited upon her. What a treasure she was, really! He resolved to protect and cherish her always.

At this time, Poonam came rushing toward them.

"Oh, hi, Poonam! Thank you for letting Zehra know about me. I had no hope she would ever forgive me, but it appears she has."

"What are you talking about, sir?" asked Poonam in some consternation.

"It’s all right, Poonam. Zehra told me you told her about what I’ve been up to after I got fired. My unselfishly anonymous good deeds, et cetera."

Poonam rolled her eyes. "Oh God, I see you don’t know. I’ll have to ask you step away, sir."

"What? You still suspect me, even after Zehra herself has forgiven me?"

Poonam drew her service pistol. "Arjun Malhotra, step away from Zehra. Now!"

Hurt and betrayed, Arjun doddered, at which point Poonam roughly pushed him aside and slapped handcuffs on Zehra.

"What? Are you mad, Poonam? Why are you cuffing my Zehra?"

"She’s the one behind the bomb blast, sir, don’t you see?"

"How? But, but, her own Kabir died?"

"She put the bomb in Kabir’s tiffin box. It wouldn’t have mattered where you assigned Kabir that day. Zehra killed Kabir."

"This can’t be true," wailed Arjun. At this point, other police officers and Ayaan arrived at the scene.

"Believe me, sir, Poonam is right," said Ayaan. "We’ve been tracking Zehra for months. I was going to get to the bottom of this, but then you showed up at her house and seemed smitten, and we thought we’d use you instead. Sorry about that, sir."

"Zehra? Why?" asked Arjun, realization finally dawning on his pained yet dignified face.

"Oh, enough with the Zehra stuff," scoffed Zehra, as the arresting officers pulled off her face mask to reveal a decidedly non-prime-time South Indian male. "I’m actually Ram Gopal Sharma, a thoroughly secular male arsonist."

The officers led Sharma off to the waiting jeep, and Arjun thought about how he would never love again.
